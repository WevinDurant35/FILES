Neurological entrainment occurs when rhythmic stimuli synchronize with the brain's internal oscillations. In the visual cortex, this can generate standing waves of neural activity. These waves create perceived patterns known as phosphenes. When these stimuli are placed in the periphery, they trigger the superior colliculus, making it nearly impossible for the prefrontal cortex to maintain sustained attention on a central task. This is the physiological basis for sensory-driven cognitive collapse.
